Historical Events — March 22
FDR Signs the Cullen-Harrison Act, Ending the Beer Drought — 1933
President Franklin Roosevelt signed the Cullen-Harrison Act on March 22, 1933, legalizing the manufacture and sale of 3.2% beer and light wines — the first crack in Prohibition and a deliberate economic stimulus during the depths of the Depression. FDR reportedly quipped, "I think this would be a good time for a beer." The law went into effect on April 7, generating an estimated $5 million in beer sales in Chicago alone on day one, and the brewing industry quickly created hundreds of thousands of jobs in production, distribution, and hospitality.
The Arab League Is Founded in Cairo — 1945
Representatives from Egypt, Syria, Lebanon, Transjordan, Saudi Arabia, Iraq, and Yemen signed the Pact of the League of Arab States in Cairo on March 22, 1945, creating the first major post-colonial regional bloc in the Middle East. The League was designed to coordinate political, economic, and military policy among member states — a mission that would put it at the center of every Arab-Israeli conflict, oil embargo, and Gulf crisis for the next eight decades. Today it counts 22 member states and remains a key voice in the geopolitics of global energy markets.
The Equal Rights Amendment Passes the U.S. Senate — 1972
The U.S. Senate voted 84-8 on March 22, 1972, to approve the Equal Rights Amendment, which stated that equality of rights shall not be denied on account of sex, and sent it to the states for ratification. The amendment had been introduced in every session of Congress since 1923 and passed the House the previous year. Despite strong initial momentum — 30 states ratified within the first year — the ERA fell three states short of the required 38 by its 1982 deadline, making it one of the most consequential near-misses in American constitutional history.
The First Stanley Cup Championship Is Played — 1894
The Montreal Hockey Club defeated the Ottawa Generals 3-1 at Victoria Skating Rink in Montreal on March 22, 1894, in the first official championship challenge for Lord Stanley's Cup — the trophy that Governor General Lord Stanley of Preston had donated the previous year. The silver bowl cost 10 guineas (about $50 at the time); today the Stanley Cup is the oldest professional sports trophy in North America and the NHL franchise that lifts it commands a valuation measured in billions.
The Beatles Release "Please Please Me" — 1963
The Beatles' debut album "Please Please Me" hit record shop shelves in the UK on March 22, 1963, with 10 of its 14 tracks famously recorded in a single marathon session on February 11. Produced by George Martin on Parlophone, the album shot to number one on the UK charts and stayed there for 30 consecutive weeks before being displaced by the band's own follow-up, "With the Beatles." The record ignited Beatlemania and launched the most commercially successful act in music history — a cultural force that reshaped the entertainment industry's economics forever.
