Historical Events — March 5
The Boston Massacre — 1770
British sentries guarding the Boston Customs House fired into a crowd of colonists, killing five including Crispus Attucks, a former slave who became the first casualty of the American Revolution. The incident was seized upon by Samuel Adams and Paul Revere to galvanize anti-British sentiment across the colonies, transforming a street brawl into a rallying cry for independence.
Churchill's "Iron Curtain" Speech — 1946
At Westminster College in Fulton, Missouri, former British Prime Minister Winston Churchill delivered his famous address declaring that an iron curtain had descended across Europe from the Baltic to the Adriatic. With President Truman sitting behind him, Churchill's blunt characterization of Soviet expansionism is widely considered the opening salvo of the Cold War.
Stalin Dies — 1953
Joseph Stalin, who ruled the Soviet Union with absolute power for nearly three decades, died in Moscow at age 74 after suffering a stroke days earlier. His death triggered a power struggle that Nikita Khrushchev would eventually win, ushering in the "thaw" and a period of de-Stalinization that reshaped Soviet politics and Cold War dynamics.
Samuel Colt Opens His First Revolver Factory — 1836
Samuel Colt established his first manufacturing plant in Paterson, New Jersey to produce his recently patented .34-caliber revolving firearm. The "Paterson" revolver introduced the concept of interchangeable parts to firearms manufacturing and laid the foundation for Colt's eventual dominance of the American arms industry — a business model that would reshape both warfare and industrial production.
Hugo Chávez Dies in Office — 2013
Venezuelan President Hugo Chávez died of cancer at 58 in Caracas after 14 years in power. His death sent oil markets into a brief volatility spike as traders priced in uncertainty over the future of Venezuelan crude exports — then the largest in the Western Hemisphere. Nicolás Maduro succeeded him, inheriting both Chávez's populist movement and a deteriorating economy.
