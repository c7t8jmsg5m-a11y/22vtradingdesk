Historical Events — March 21
America's First Open-End Mutual Fund Is Born — 1924
Massachusetts Investors Trust launched on March 21, 1924, becoming the first open-ended mutual fund in the United States — an innovation that let ordinary investors pool capital and redeem shares at net asset value for the first time. The fund started with $50,000 in assets and 200 shareholders; by the 1950s it was one of the largest funds in the country. MIT's structure became the blueprint for an industry that today manages over $25 trillion in U.S. assets alone, democratizing access to markets that had been the exclusive domain of the wealthy.
The Sharpeville Massacre — 1960
South African police opened fire on a crowd of unarmed Black demonstrators protesting the apartheid regime's pass laws in the township of Sharpeville, killing 69 people and wounding 180 — many shot in the back as they fled. The massacre triggered worldwide condemnation, a wave of capital flight from South Africa, and the United Nations' first resolution against apartheid. It marked the moment the anti-apartheid struggle shifted from peaceful protest toward armed resistance, and March 21 is now observed internationally as the UN's International Day for the Elimination of Racial Discrimination.
Germany Launches Operation Michael — 1918
The German army unleashed its massive Spring Offensive along a 50-mile front near the Somme River in France, hurling 76 divisions and over one million men at Allied lines in a last-ditch bid to win World War I before American reinforcements could tip the balance. The assault punched a 40-mile hole in British defenses in five days — the biggest territorial gain on the Western Front since 1914. But the offensive ultimately exhausted Germany's reserves, and within six months the war was over.
UCLA Caps a Perfect 30-0 Season with Wooden's First Title — 1964
UCLA routed Duke 98-83 in Kansas City to complete a flawless 30-0 season and deliver John Wooden the first of his record ten NCAA basketball championships. The Bruins had no player taller than 6-foot-5 and relied on Wooden's suffocating full-court press — a system so dominant it would produce seven consecutive titles starting in 1967. The win launched the most remarkable dynasty in college sports history and cemented Wooden as the gold standard for coaching excellence.
Jack Dorsey Sends the First Tweet — 2006
Twitter co-founder Jack Dorsey posted the platform's first message — "just setting up my twttr" — launching what would become one of the most influential social media networks in history. Twitter's real-time format reshaped everything from breaking news to presidential communication, and the company went public in November 2013 at a $31 billion valuation. In 2022, Elon Musk acquired the platform for $44 billion and rebranded it as X, capping one of the most dramatic corporate sagas in tech history.
