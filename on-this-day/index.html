Historical Events — March 17
Caesar Wins the Battle of Munda — 45 BC
Julius Caesar defeated the last Pompeian army at Munda in southern Spain, ending a four-year civil war and leaving him the undisputed master of Rome. The battle was among the bloodiest of the late Republic — some 30,000 Pompeian soldiers fell — and Caesar reportedly told friends it was the first time he had fought for his life. His unchallenged power led directly to the declaration of dictator perpetuo and, within a year, his assassination on the Ides of March.
British Forces Evacuate Boston — 1776
General George Washington's overnight placement of cannons on Dorchester Heights — artillery hauled 300 miles from Fort Ticonderoga by Henry Knox — forced British General William Howe to abandon Boston without firing a shot. Some 9,000 British troops and 1,100 Loyalists sailed for Halifax, ending an eleven-month siege. The date is still celebrated in Boston as Evacuation Day, a local holiday that conveniently doubles as St. Patrick's Day.
National Gallery of Art Opens in Washington — 1941
President Franklin Roosevelt formally dedicated the National Gallery of Art, built entirely with a $15 million gift from Treasury Secretary Andrew Mellon, who also donated his personal collection of 121 Old Master paintings and 21 sculptures to seed it. Mellon, one of America's wealthiest financiers and a former head of Alcoa, Gulf Oil, and the Mellon Bank empire, conceived the gallery as a national institution to rival the Louvre. He died in 1937 and never saw the building completed.
Golda Meir Elected Prime Minister of Israel — 1969
The Israeli Labor Party elected 70-year-old Golda Meir as party leader, making her the country's fourth prime minister and one of the first female heads of government in the modern world. A Milwaukee-raised former schoolteacher turned diplomat, Meir inherited a nation still absorbing its 1967 territorial gains and would lead Israel through the shock of the 1973 Yom Kippur War. She remains the only woman to have held the post.
White South Africans Vote to End Apartheid — 1992
In a whites-only referendum, 68.7% of South African voters backed President F.W. de Klerk's reforms to dismantle apartheid, clearing the path for multi-racial elections in 1994. The decisive margin stunned hardliners and signaled to global markets that South Africa was open for business again. International sanctions were lifted within months, and foreign capital flooded back into the Johannesburg Stock Exchange after years of isolation.
