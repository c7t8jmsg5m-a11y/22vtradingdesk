Historical Events — March 11
WHO Declares COVID-19 a Pandemic — S&P 500 Craters 9.5% the Next Day — 2020
The World Health Organization officially declared COVID-19 a global pandemic, with more than 118,000 cases across 114 countries. The declaration accelerated an already vicious sell-off: the S&P 500 fell 9.5% the following day in its worst session since 1987, and the index would bottom 23% lower just eight trading days later. Within weeks, the Fed cut rates to zero and Congress passed a $2.2 trillion relief package — setting in motion the liquidity-fueled bull market and inflation cycle that defined the next five years of markets.
FDR Signs the Lend-Lease Act — 1941
President Franklin Roosevelt signed the Lend-Lease Act, authorizing the United States to supply weapons, food, and materiel to Allied nations fighting the Axis powers — nine months before Pearl Harbor officially brought America into the war. The program ultimately channeled over $50 billion (roughly $700 billion in today’s dollars) to 30 countries, transforming the US into the “arsenal of democracy” and turbocharging American industrial output. It marked the definitive end of US isolationism and laid the groundwork for the postwar economic boom.
The Tohoku Earthquake and Fukushima Nuclear Disaster — 2011
A 9.0-magnitude earthquake struck off the coast of northeastern Japan, triggering a massive tsunami that killed nearly 20,000 people and caused a triple meltdown at the Fukushima Daiichi nuclear power plant — the worst nuclear accident since Chernobyl. The disaster caused an estimated $235 billion in damage, making it the costliest natural disaster in history. Japan shut down its entire nuclear fleet and pivoted hard toward LNG imports, reshaping energy trade flows across Asia for a decade.
The First NHL Championship Game Is Played — 1918
The Toronto Arenas defeated the Montreal Canadiens 7-3 in the first-ever NHL championship game, played at Mutual Street Arena in Toronto. It was the inaugural season of the National Hockey League, which had been formed just four months earlier with four teams after the collapse of its predecessor, the National Hockey Association. The Arenas went on to win the Stanley Cup that year, beating the PCHA’s Vancouver Millionaires — the beginning of what would become North America’s oldest professional sports trophy tradition.
A Raisin in the Sun Opens on Broadway — 1959
Lorraine Hansberry’s A Raisin in the Sun premiered at the Ethel Barrymore Theatre, making her the first Black woman to have a play produced on Broadway. The story of a working-class Black family on Chicago’s South Side hoping to improve their lives struck a nerve with audiences and critics alike, running for 530 performances. Sidney Poitier starred as Walter Lee Younger in a role that cemented his status as one of America’s leading actors.
