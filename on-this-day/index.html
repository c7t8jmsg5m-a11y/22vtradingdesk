Historical Events — March 15
The Assassination of Julius Caesar — 44 BC
Roman dictator Julius Caesar was stabbed to death in the Senate house by a conspiracy of roughly 60 senators led by Marcus Junius Brutus and Gaius Cassius Longinus, who believed they were saving the Republic from tyranny. Caesar had been warned by a soothsayer to beware the Ides of March — a date Shakespeare would later immortalize — but walked into the Senate unarmed. Far from restoring the Republic, the assassination triggered a series of civil wars that ultimately ended it, ushering in the Roman Empire under Caesar's adopted heir Octavian.
Czar Nicholas II Abdicates the Russian Throne — 1917
Under pressure from Petrograd insurgents and his own generals, Czar Nicholas II signed his abdication papers aboard a railway car at Pskov, ending the 304-year Romanov dynasty and centuries of imperial rule in Russia. The provisional government that replaced him would itself last only months before the Bolsheviks seized power in October. Nicholas and his family were held captive and executed by firing squad in July 1918 — a sequence of events that redrew the geopolitical map of the 20th century and created the Soviet Union.
Symbolics.com Becomes the First Registered .com Domain — 1985
Symbolics, Inc., a small computer manufacturer spun out of the MIT Artificial Intelligence Lab, registered symbolics.com — the first .com domain name in the history of the internet. In 1985, registering a domain meant emailing the Stanford Research Institute and waiting days for manual approval; only five other companies would register a .com that entire year. Today there are over 350 million registered domain names worldwide, and the .com namespace alone underpins a multi-trillion-dollar digital economy that was unimaginable when a Lisp machine company quietly claimed the first address.
The Cincinnati Red Stockings Become Baseball's First Professional Team — 1869
Cincinnati attorney Aaron Champion hired English-born cricket player Harry Wright to organize, manage, and play center field for the Cincinnati Red Stockings, establishing the first openly all-salaried professional baseball club. The National Association of Baseball Players had only recently lifted its ban on paying players, and Wright assembled a roster of ten men at salaries ranging from $600 to $1,400 for the season. The Red Stockings went on an undefeated 57-0 run that year, proving the commercial viability of professional sport and laying the foundation for what would become Major League Baseball.
Elizabeth Taylor and Richard Burton Wed in Montreal — 1964
Elizabeth Taylor and Richard Burton married in a private ceremony at the Ritz-Carlton Hotel in Montreal, with only nine guests present and paparazzi scaling walls outside to catch a glimpse. Their affair, ignited on the set of Cleopatra, had already consumed global tabloid attention for two years — Burton called it "Le Scandale" — and this was Taylor's fifth marriage, his second. The couple would divorce, remarry in Botswana, and divorce again, but their union defined celebrity culture for a generation and remains one of Hollywood's most storied romances.
