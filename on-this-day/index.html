Historical Events — March 17
White South Africans Vote to End Apartheid — 1992
In a whites-only referendum, 68.7% of South African voters backed President F.W. de Klerk's reforms to dismantle apartheid, effectively ending decades of institutionalized racial segregation. The decisive mandate cleared the path for multi-racial elections in 1994 and the lifting of international sanctions that had frozen South Africa out of global capital markets. The Johannesburg Stock Exchange surged in the months that followed as foreign investment flooded back into the country.
British Forces Evacuate Boston — 1776
General George Washington's bold overnight placement of cannons on Dorchester Heights — artillery hauled 300 miles from Fort Ticonderoga by Henry Knox — forced British General William Howe to abandon Boston without firing a shot. Some 9,000 British troops and 1,100 Loyalists sailed for Halifax, Nova Scotia, ending an eleven-month siege. The date is still celebrated in Boston as Evacuation Day, a local holiday that conveniently doubles as St. Patrick's Day.
Vanguard 1 Becomes the First Solar-Powered Satellite — 1958
The U.S. Navy launched Vanguard 1 from Cape Canaveral, a grapefruit-sized, 3.2-pound satellite that became the first spacecraft powered by solar cells. Its solar-powered transmitter outlasted its battery-powered one by years, proving that photovoltaic technology could reliably operate in space. Vanguard 1 remains in orbit today — the oldest human-made object still circling the Earth — and its success helped catalyze the solar energy industry that now generates over $400 billion in annual revenue globally.
Bob Cousy's Tearful Farewell at Boston Garden — 1963
The Boston Celtics honored point guard Bob Cousy in an emotional ceremony at Boston Garden before his final regular-season home game. When Cousy broke down mid-speech, a fan shouted "We love ya, Cooz!" and the crowd of 13,909 roared for a full minute. Cousy, the NBA's first true playmaker and a six-time champion, retired at 34 after leading the Celtics dynasty that would win eight straight titles.
Erin Brockovich Opens, Julia Roberts Commands $20 Million — 2000
Universal Pictures released Erin Brockovich, the true story of a legal clerk who built a landmark environmental contamination case against Pacific Gas & Electric that resulted in a $333 million settlement — the largest direct-action lawsuit payout in U.S. history at the time. Julia Roberts became the first actress ever to earn $20 million for a single film, a milestone that reshaped Hollywood pay scales. The film grossed $256 million worldwide and won Roberts the Academy Award for Best Actress.
