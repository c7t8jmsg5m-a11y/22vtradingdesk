Historical Events — March 6
The Fall of the Alamo — 1836
After a 13-day siege, Mexican General Antonio López de Santa Anna's forces stormed the Alamo mission in San Antonio, killing nearly all of the roughly 200 Texan defenders — including William Travis, James Bowie, and Davy Crockett. The battle became a rallying cry for Texas independence. Within six weeks, Sam Houston's forces defeated Santa Anna at San Jacinto shouting "Remember the Alamo!" — and the Republic of Texas was born. The territory would join the United States nine years later.
The Dred Scott Decision — 1857
The U.S. Supreme Court ruled 7-2 in Dred Scott v. Sandford that enslaved people were not U.S. citizens and could not sue for their freedom in federal court. Chief Justice Roger Taney went further, declaring the Missouri Compromise unconstitutional and ruling that Congress had no authority to ban slavery in any territory. The decision deepened the national divide over slavery and is widely considered one of the catalysts of the Civil War, which began four years later.
Mendeleev Presents the Periodic Table — 1869
Russian chemist Dmitri Mendeleev presented his periodic table of elements to the Russian Chemical Society in St. Petersburg, organizing 63 known elements by atomic weight and chemical properties. His system left gaps for elements not yet discovered — and when gallium, scandium, and germanium were subsequently found, they matched his predictions almost exactly. The periodic table remains the organizing framework of modern chemistry, one of the most powerful predictive models in all of science.
Ghana Gains Independence — 1957
The Gold Coast became the first sub-Saharan African nation to gain independence from colonial rule, renamed Ghana under the leadership of Kwame Nkrumah. The event electrified independence movements across Africa — within a decade, more than 30 African nations would follow. Nkrumah envisioned Ghana as a model for Pan-African unity and economic self-determination, though his presidency later descended into authoritarianism before a military coup in 1966.
Cassius Clay Becomes Muhammad Ali — 1964
Heavyweight boxing champion Cassius Clay announced he was taking a new name given to him by Nation of Islam leader Elijah Muhammad: Muhammad Ali. The name change came days after his stunning upset of Sonny Liston in Miami Beach and represented Ali's embrace of the Nation of Islam — a decision that made him one of the most polarizing and ultimately beloved figures of the 20th century. His refusal to be drafted for the Vietnam War three years later would cost him his title and cement his legacy as a cultural icon far beyond the ring.
