Historical Events — March 16
Bear Stearns Sold to JPMorgan Chase for $2 a Share — 2008
After burning through nearly all of its $18 billion cash reserves in a single week, Bear Stearns — the nation's fifth-largest investment bank with $400 billion in assets — accepted a fire-sale offer from JPMorgan Chase at $2 per share, a 93% discount from its Friday close. The Fed backstopped the deal by absorbing $30 billion of Bear's toxic mortgage-backed securities through a newly created vehicle called Maiden Lane LLC. The collapse of the 85-year-old firm was the first domino of the financial crisis, foreshadowing the failures of Lehman Brothers and AIG six months later.
Robert Goddard Launches the First Liquid-Fueled Rocket — 1926
From a snow-covered farm in Auburn, Massachusetts, physicist Robert H. Goddard launched a small gasoline-and-liquid-oxygen rocket that flew for 2.5 seconds, reached 41 feet, and landed 184 feet away — the first successful flight of a liquid-fueled rocket in history. The New York Times had mocked Goddard's theories as lacking "the knowledge ladled out daily in high schools," but his work laid the foundation for every space launch that followed. NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center bears his name today.
Congress Establishes West Point — 1802
President Thomas Jefferson signed legislation creating the United States Military Academy at West Point, New York, on a strategic bluff overlooking the Hudson River that had served as a garrison since 1778. Originally founded to train the Army Corps of Engineers, the academy would produce generals from Robert E. Lee and Ulysses S. Grant to Dwight Eisenhower and Douglas MacArthur. West Point remains the oldest continuously occupied military post in the United States.
Maurice "Rocket" Richard Suspended, Sparking the Richard Riot — 1955
NHL president Clarence Campbell suspended Montreal Canadiens superstar Maurice "Rocket" Richard for the remainder of the regular season and the entire playoffs after Richard struck a linesman during a game. When Campbell showed up at the Montreal Forum the following night, fans pelted him with eggs and tomatoes before a tear-gas canister sent 15,000 spectators into the streets, igniting a full-scale riot across downtown Montreal. The Richard Riot is widely regarded as an early flashpoint of Quebec's Quiet Revolution.
Nathaniel Hawthorne's The Scarlet Letter Published — 1850
Ticknor, Reed, and Fields of Boston published Nathaniel Hawthorne's The Scarlet Letter, a novel set in Puritan Massachusetts that explored guilt, sin, and public shame through the story of Hester Prynne. The first printing of 2,500 copies sold out in ten days — a remarkable pace for the era — and the book became one of the first mass bestsellers in American publishing. It cemented Hawthorne as a major literary figure and remains a staple of the American canon nearly two centuries later.
