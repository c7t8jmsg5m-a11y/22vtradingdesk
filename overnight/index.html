Bottom Line
The overnight tape is cautiously risk-off heading into the first triple witching of 2026, with $5.7 trillion in notional options set to expire — the largest March expiry on record per Citigroup data. The Fed's hawkish hold on Wednesday continues to reverberate: the dot plot now signals just one cut for 2026, rate-cut expectations have been fully removed from the May meeting, and Chair Powell explicitly flagged Iran-driven energy costs as a risk to the 2% inflation target. Oil remains the dominant macro variable, with Brent above $110 and WTI near $96 as Iran's Strait of Hormuz threats keep a floor under crude — though Treasury Secretary Bessent's comments about unsanctioning 140 million barrels of Iranian oil on the water offered a brief reprieve. Gold's 6% weekly collapse is the clearest sign of forced deleveraging: the metal crashed through $5,000 into the $4,650s, its worst week since the pandemic. FDX's blowout after-hours beat ($5.25 vs. $4.16 est.) and raised guidance provide a rare economic bright spot. Stay nimble through the witching hour; the closing auction will be treacherous. Lean defensive on any post-expiry rally attempt.

■ Price vs. Narrative Gap
Gold's violent sell-off screams "risk-off" but the driver is pure positioning mechanics — leveraged paper longs getting margin-called — not a fundamental shift in the safe-haven thesis. Physical gold premiums remain elevated and central bank buying continues unabated. Meanwhile, the equity market's reaction to the Fed hold was disproportionately negative given Powell's upbeat growth commentary; the tape is pricing in stagflation while the data (Philly Fed, FDX) says the real economy is holding up better than sentiment. The convergence trade: buy gold on further paper weakness, fade recession panic in transports.

But the Philly Fed's 18.1 print and FDX's raised guidance argue the economy hasn't cracked. Markets are pricing the worst-case before the data confirms it.
