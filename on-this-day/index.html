Historical Events — March 9
FDR Submits the Emergency Banking Act — 1933
Four days into his presidency and with over 4,000 banks already shuttered, Franklin D. Roosevelt sent the Emergency Banking Act to Congress, which passed it in under eight hours — many members never even read the final text. The law gave the Treasury authority to inspect and reopen solvent banks, effectively backstopping the financial system. When banks reopened on March 13, deposits exceeded withdrawals for the first time in weeks, marking the psychological turning point of the Great Depression's banking panic.
The Battle of the Ironclads at Hampton Roads — 1862
The USS Monitor and CSS Virginia (rebuilt from the captured USS Merrimack) clashed for five hours in the waters off Hampton Roads, Virginia, fighting to a draw in the first battle between iron-armored warships. The engagement rendered every wooden navy in the world instantly obsolete — Britain alone had 149 ships of the line that were now strategically worthless. Naval powers rushed to build ironclad fleets, igniting an arms race that reshaped global defense spending for the rest of the century.
Operation Meetinghouse: The Firebombing of Tokyo — 1945
Over 300 American B-29 bombers dropped nearly 1,700 tons of incendiary bombs on Tokyo in the deadliest single air raid in history. The resulting firestorm destroyed 16 square miles of the city, killed an estimated 100,000 civilians, and left one million homeless in a single night. The raid demonstrated that strategic bombing alone could cripple an industrial economy — Japan's war production never recovered, and the nation's six largest industrial cities were 40% destroyed by war's end.
Chrysler Acquires American Motors Corporation — 1987
Chrysler announced its $1.5 billion acquisition of American Motors Corporation, absorbing the struggling automaker and — crucially — gaining full ownership of the Jeep brand. AMC had been losing money for years and was 46% owned by Renault, which was eager to exit. The deal proved transformational: Jeep became Chrysler's most valuable franchise, and the SUV boom of the 1990s turned what looked like a bailout purchase into one of the great brand acquisitions in automotive history.
Barbie Makes Her Debut at the Toy Fair — 1959
Mattel co-founder Ruth Handler introduced the Barbie doll at the American International Toy Fair in New York, priced at $3. Toy buyers were skeptical — no one had tried selling an adult-figured fashion doll to children before — but 300,000 units sold in the first year. Barbie went on to become the best-selling fashion doll in history, generating over $1.5 billion in annual revenue for Mattel and anchoring a consumer products empire that spans 150+ countries.
