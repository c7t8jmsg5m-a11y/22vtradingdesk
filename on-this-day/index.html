Historical Events — March 8
The New York Stock Exchange Is Born — 1817
A group of brokers who had been trading informally in coffeehouses along Wall Street adopted a constitution and renamed themselves the New York Stock & Exchange Board, renting a room at 40 Wall Street for $200 a month. The exchange listed just 30 securities and traded them via a twice-daily roll call from the president's podium — the origin of the term "seat" for an exchange membership. Today the NYSE lists over 2,800 companies with a combined market capitalization exceeding $28 trillion.
The February Revolution Erupts in Petrograd — 1917
Rioting broke out in Petrograd (now St. Petersburg) as workers and soldiers took to the streets over bread shortages and war exhaustion, marking the start of the February Revolution. Within nine days, Tsar Nicholas II abdicated, ending three centuries of Romanov rule. The power vacuum that followed set the stage for the Bolshevik seizure of power in October — a geopolitical shock whose effects on global markets and commodity flows persisted for the rest of the century.
Commodore Perry Opens Treaty Negotiations with Japan — 1854
With his squadron anchored off Yokohama, Commodore Matthew C. Perry went ashore to begin formal treaty talks with Japanese officials. On March 31 he signed the Treaty of Kanagawa, prying open 200 years of Japanese isolation and establishing America's first commercial foothold in the Pacific. The treaty laid the groundwork for the U.S.-Japan trade relationship that would become one of the largest bilateral economic partnerships in history.
Ali vs. Frazier: The Fight of the Century — 1971
Joe Frazier defeated Muhammad Ali by unanimous decision in 15 rounds at Madison Square Garden, handing Ali his first professional loss before a sold-out crowd of 20,455. The bout was the first time two undefeated heavyweight champions had met, and ringside seats sold for a then-unprecedented $150. Closed-circuit television rights generated $20 million in revenue, pioneering the pay-per-view model that would transform the economics of professional sports.
Malaysia Airlines Flight 370 Vanishes — 2014
Malaysia Airlines Flight 370, a Boeing 777 carrying 239 passengers and crew from Kuala Lumpur to Beijing, disappeared from radar 38 minutes after takeoff and was never found. The ensuing multinational search became the most expensive in aviation history, spanning over 46,000 square miles of the southern Indian Ocean. The disappearance triggered an overhaul of global flight-tracking standards and sent Malaysia Airlines into a financial tailspin that required a full government-backed restructuring.
