Historical Events — March 10
Alexander Graham Bell Makes the First Telephone Call — 1876
Alexander Graham Bell transmitted the words “Mr. Watson, come here — I want to see you” to his assistant Thomas Watson in the next room, completing the first successful telephone call just three days after receiving his patent. Bell wrote his father that evening predicting telegraph wires would one day be “laid on to houses just like water and gas.” The invention spawned AT&T and the modern telecommunications industry — a sector that today represents over $1.7 trillion in global revenue.
The NASDAQ Hits Its Dot-Com Peak — 2000
The NASDAQ Composite touched an intraday high of 5,048.62, marking the absolute zenith of the dot-com bubble. The index had surged over 500% in five years, driven by frenzied speculation in internet stocks with little or no earnings. From this peak, the NASDAQ would lose 78% of its value over the next two and a half years, wiping out roughly $5 trillion in market capitalization and reshaping venture capital, IPO markets, and tech investing for a generation.
The Tibetan Uprising Begins in Lhasa — 1959
Fearing that Chinese authorities planned to abduct the Dalai Lama, an estimated 300,000 Tibetans surrounded his Norbulingka Palace in Lhasa, igniting a full-scale revolt against Chinese rule. The uprising was crushed within days by the People’s Liberation Army, killing tens of thousands, and the Dalai Lama fled across the Himalayas to India, where he established a government-in-exile that persists to this day. The anniversary is commemorated annually as Tibetan Uprising Day and remains one of the most politically sensitive dates on Beijing’s calendar.
Silicon Valley Bank Collapses — 2023
Silicon Valley Bank was seized by the FDIC after a classic bank run drained $42 billion in deposits in a single day, making it the second-largest bank failure in U.S. history. SVB had loaded up on long-duration Treasuries during the zero-rate era, and rising rates blew a $17 billion hole in its bond portfolio. The collapse triggered a brief regional banking crisis, forced emergency Fed lending facilities, and became a case study in duration risk and social-media-accelerated contagion.
The Courrières Mine Disaster Kills 1,099 — 1906
An underground coal dust explosion tore through a network of mines near Lens in northern France, killing 1,099 workers in the deadliest mining disaster in European history. Rescue efforts were chaotic — incredibly, 13 survivors were pulled from the rubble after 20 days underground. The catastrophe triggered a wave of miners’ strikes across France involving 60,000 workers and accelerated landmark workplace safety legislation that reshaped European labor law for the industrial age.
