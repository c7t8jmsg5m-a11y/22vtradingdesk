Market Overview

SPX gapped lower at the open and sold through its 5-day MA of 6,872, trading down to 6,836 by midday with no meaningful bounce. The tape feels heavy — the index is fighting two-sided headwinds from tariff escalation and the private credit complex unwind. Friday's Supreme Court decision striking down Trump's reciprocal tariffs briefly sparked a relief bid, but the weekend announcement of a new 15% global tariff under IEEPA immediately replaced one policy risk with another. Europe warned the tariffs could jeopardize trade deals, adding geopolitical uncertainty to the mix.

NDX is tracking in line with SPX, off 1.16%, but the damage is concentrated in high-multiple software — CRWD −9.5%, DDOG −9.6%, WDAY −8.3%, INTU −7.9%, NOW −4.3%, ORCL −6.0%. Enterprise software is now deep in correction territory on mounting AI-disruption fears; roughly 20% of private credit's loan book over the past decade has gone to software companies, and that cross-read is hitting both sectors simultaneously. DJIA lagging badly at −1.57%, dragged by AXP −8.1% on consumer spend fears and JPM −4.7% in the financial selloff. RTY worst in show at −2.11%, confirming the risk-off sweep is broadest in the most cyclical, most domestically exposed corner of the market.

Key Single-Stock Narratives
KKR −9.2%  |  BX −7.8%  |  ARES −8.0%  |  APO −6.6%

The alt manager complex is extending what's become a multi-week rout, now accelerated by the Blue Owl OBDC II redemption gate that spooked the entire private credit space last week. KKR is down nearly 16% YTD after Q4 profit declines and AI-exposure concerns. BX −7.8% despite being the most diversified of the group — the contagion is indiscriminate. The math is brutal: roughly 20% of private credit loans over the past decade went to software companies, and with AI now threatening that revenue base, the dual risk of credit quality deterioration + software disruption is repricing the entire sector. APO reportedly profited from short positions against First Brands' debt, but the sector overhang is overwhelming individual stories. Investors reassessing liquidity, valuation transparency, and systemic risk across the $1.8T private credit market.

LLY +5.1% — Novo CagriSema Trial Failure

LLY surging after Novo Nordisk's next-gen obesity drug CagriSema failed to demonstrate non-inferiority to Lilly's tirzepatide (Zepbound) in the REDEFINE 4 trial. CagriSema delivered 20.2% weight loss vs 23.6% for tirzepatide on the treatment-regimen estimand — a decisive miss. Novo's stock plunged 15%, wiping ~$100B in market cap. For Lilly, this validates Zepbound as the gold standard and solidifies its competitive moat heading into the orforglipron pill launch in Q2 2026. Morgan Stanley called it a "worst-case scenario" for Novo. Lilly now trades at $1,061 with guided 2026 revenue growth of 25%. The GLP-1 market has shifted from "rising tide lifts all boats" to "winner takes most."

CRWD −9.5%  |  DDOG −9.6%  |  WDAY −8.3% — Software Implosion

Enterprise software is in free-fall as the market reprices the AI-disruption thesis. CRWD trading at 2.1x avg vol on the decline to RSI 29, approaching deeply oversold territory. WDAY now at RSI 17 — historically extreme. INTU RSI 18. The fear is that AI agents will displace traditional SaaS seat-based licensing, compressing multiples for the entire cohort. The cross-read from private credit — 20% of leveraged loan exposure to software — means the financial system is also a forced seller. CRM −5.2% ahead of its own earnings this week adds urgency to the de-risking. The question into the close: is this capitulation or just the beginning of a broader re-rating?

GLW +5.0% — AI Fiber Momentum Continues

GLW extends its remarkable 2026 run (+50% YTD) after the $6B Meta fiber-optic deal and UBS's aggressive PT raise to $160. The "Scale Up" thesis — replacing copper inside server racks with fiber — is now consensus, with UBS estimating that market could be 2-3x larger than current Scale Out demand. Optical Communications grew 24% YoY in Q4 with enterprise sales jumping 58%. RSI at 78 signals overbought but momentum is undeniable as hyperscaler capex continues to accelerate. Corning has become the toll-booth of the AI infrastructure buildout.

MRK +1.0% — Keytruda Unit Split

MRK announced it's splitting its Human Health division into a standalone oncology unit (housing Keytruda, $31.7B in 2025 sales) and a separate specialty/pharma/infectious disease unit. This restructuring is widely read as a precursor to a potential future spinoff, giving Merck "more optionality" ahead of Keytruda's 2028 U.S. patent cliff. Brian Foard from Sanofi hired to run the specialty unit. The market reaction is muted — shares up ~1% — but the strategic implications are significant. Merck has tripled its pipeline since 2021 and struck two ~$10B deals last year.

Private Credit Contagion Watch
Blue Owl's OBDC II redemption gate — ending quarterly liquidity and shifting to periodic distributions — has become the catalyst for a broader repricing of the $1.8T private credit market. The concern is not one fund but the structural question: if rates stay higher for longer under tariff-driven inflation, and AI disrupts the 20% of leveraged loans exposed to software, who holds the risk? Alt managers down 12-18% YTD. Watch for: second-order fund gates, BDC NAV markdowns, and CLO tranching stress. This is the new macro fault line.

Bottom Line

This is a tape under pressure from multiple directions — tariffs providing the macro headwind, private credit providing the systemic stress, and AI disruption providing the sector catalyst. The 1:2.7 breadth ratio with only Utilities green tells you this is indiscriminate selling, not rotation. The alt manager meltdown is the story that has legs — Blue Owl's redemption gate opened a Pandora's box that won't close until the market has full visibility on BDC liquidity and software loan exposure. NVDA Wednesday is the swing factor: a blowout could stabilize tech and give the tape a reason to bid, while anything less than perfection risks extending the correction into March. Stay defensive, own quality, and don't catch knives in software or alt managers until the forced selling exhausts itself. Let NVDA decide.
