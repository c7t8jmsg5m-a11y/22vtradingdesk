Historical Events — March 18
COVID Crash Circuit Breakers Fire for the Fourth Time — 2020
The S&P 500 plunged 5% and the Dow Jones Industrial Average cratered more than 6% as pandemic panic gripped global markets, triggering the fourth circuit breaker halt in just eight trading sessions. The VIX had spiked above 80 — levels not seen since the depths of '08 — and the Fed had already emergency-cut rates to zero two days prior. March 18, 2020 marked the low-water point of a 34% drawdown from the February peak, though the fastest bear market in history would find its bottom just five sessions later.
British Parliament Repeals the Stamp Act — 1766
Under intense pressure from colonial boycotts and London merchants losing transatlantic revenue, Parliament repealed the Stamp Act — the first direct tax Britain had levied on American colonists. The Act had sparked riots, the formation of the Sons of Liberty, and a coordinated embargo of British goods that hammered British exporters. Parliament saved face by simultaneously passing the Declaratory Act, asserting its right to tax the colonies however it pleased — setting the stage for the conflicts that led to revolution a decade later.
Alexei Leonov Performs the First Spacewalk — 1965
Soviet cosmonaut Alexei Leonov floated outside the Voskhod 2 capsule for 12 minutes, becoming the first human to walk in space. His suit ballooned in the vacuum and he nearly couldn't squeeze back through the airlock, forcing him to bleed pressure from the suit in a split-second decision that could have killed him. The feat extended the Soviet lead in the space race and pushed NASA to accelerate the Gemini program, fueling the aerospace spending boom that ran through the rest of the decade.
Lord Stanley Donates the Stanley Cup — 1892
Canada's Governor General, Lord Frederick Arthur Stanley, announced the donation of a silver trophy to be awarded annually to the country's best amateur hockey team. The bowl cost 10 guineas — roughly $50 at the time — and today the Stanley Cup is the oldest professional sports trophy in North America, presiding over an NHL that generates north of $6 billion in annual revenue. No piece of hardware in sports has a better cost-to-cultural-impact ratio.
The Gardner Museum Heist — 1990
Two men dressed as Boston police officers talked their way into the Isabella Stewart Gardner Museum in the early morning hours and walked out with 13 works of art worth an estimated $500 million — including Vermeer's "The Concert" and three Rembrandts. It remains the largest property theft in history and not a single piece has been recovered, despite a $10 million reward. The empty frames still hang on the museum's walls, a policy mandated by Gardner's will that has become one of the most haunting images in the art world.
