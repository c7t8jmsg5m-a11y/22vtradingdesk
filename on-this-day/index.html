Historical Events — March 12
Bernie Madoff Pleads Guilty to Largest Ponzi Scheme in History — 2009
Bernard Madoff stood before a packed Manhattan courtroom and pleaded guilty to 11 federal felonies, admitting he had defrauded thousands of investors of an estimated $65 billion in the largest financial fraud Wall Street had ever seen. Judge Denny Chin immediately revoked bail and ordered the 70-year-old former NASDAQ chairman into custody. Madoff received a 150-year sentence that June — and the scandal triggered sweeping regulatory reforms at the SEC, which had missed multiple red flags over two decades.
Gandhi Begins the Salt March — 1930
Mahatma Gandhi and a small group of followers departed Sabarmati Ashram on a 240-mile march to the coastal village of Dandi to produce salt from seawater — a direct challenge to the British monopoly that taxed a dietary staple. Hundreds joined along the route, and by year’s end more than 60,000 Indians had been arrested for breaking salt laws across the country. The march became the defining act of civil disobedience in the Indian independence movement and drew global attention to the human cost of colonial rule.
FDR Delivers His First Fireside Chat — 1933
Eight days into his presidency and in the teeth of the Great Depression, Franklin Roosevelt addressed the nation by radio for the first time, calmly explaining in plain language why he had ordered a nationwide bank holiday and what would happen when banks reopened. An estimated 60 million Americans tuned in, and when banks opened the next morning, deposits exceeded withdrawals for the first time in weeks. The fireside chats became Roosevelt’s signature tool for restoring public confidence — and a masterclass in crisis communication that leaders still study today.
Darryl Strawberry Is Born — 1962
Darryl Strawberry, the towering slugger who would become the face of the 1986 World Champion New York Mets, was born in Los Angeles. Selected first overall in the 1980 MLB Draft, Strawberry won Rookie of the Year in 1983 and went on to hit 335 career home runs across stints with the Mets, Dodgers, Giants, and Yankees. His raw talent was considered generational, though off-field struggles would ultimately define his story as much as the tape-measure blasts that once electrified Shea Stadium.
Coca-Cola Is Sold in Bottles for the First Time — 1894
Joseph Biedenharn, a candy store owner in Vicksburg, Mississippi, installed bottling equipment in the back of his shop and became the first person to sell Coca-Cola in glass bottles. Company owner Asa Griggs Candler was unimpressed — he later sold the national bottling rights for a single dollar and never collected even that. The contract locked in a 5-cent price with no expiration date, a legal oversight that held the price of a Coke steady for 65 years and helped build one of the most valuable consumer brands on earth.
