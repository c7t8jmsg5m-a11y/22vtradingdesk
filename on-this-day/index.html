Historical Events — March 7
Alexander Graham Bell Patents the Telephone — 1876
At 29 years old, Alexander Graham Bell received U.S. Patent No. 174,465 for his "improvement in telegraphy" — the device he called the telephone. The patent was filed just hours before a competing application from Elisha Gray, sparking one of the most famous intellectual property disputes in American history. Bell's invention spawned AT&T and reshaped global commerce, creating what was for decades the most valuable company on Earth.
Hitler Remilitarizes the Rhineland — 1936
Adolf Hitler sent 20,000 Wehrmacht troops into the demilitarized Rhineland, violating both the Treaty of Versailles and the Locarno Pact. His generals had orders to retreat if France mobilized — but the gamble paid off when neither France nor Britain lifted a finger. Historians regard the Western non-response as the moment that emboldened Hitler's expansionist agenda and set the course toward World War II.
Bloody Sunday in Selma — 1965
Six hundred civil rights marchers led by John Lewis and Hosea Williams were attacked by Alabama state troopers with tear gas and billy clubs at the Edmund Pettus Bridge. ABC interrupted its broadcast of "Judgment at Nuremberg" to air the footage, creating one of the most powerful visual juxtapositions in television history. The national outrage led directly to the passage of the Voting Rights Act five months later.
Daniel Webster's "Seventh of March" Speech — 1850
Senator Daniel Webster of Massachusetts delivered a three-hour address endorsing the Compromise of 1850, hoping to avert civil war by satisfying both North and South on the slavery question. The speech cost Webster his political base among abolitionists who branded him a traitor, but it helped delay Southern secession by a decade — buying the Union critical time to industrialize before the inevitable conflict.
U.S. Troops Capture the Bridge at Remagen — 1945
Soldiers from the 9th Armored Division seized the Ludendorff Bridge at Remagen — the last standing bridge over the Rhine — before German engineers could destroy it. The capture allowed Allied forces to pour into the German heartland weeks ahead of Eisenhower's timeline. Hitler was so enraged that he ordered the execution of the officers who failed to demolish it.
