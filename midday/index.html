The selling is methodical, not panicked — volume running about 0.3–0.5x the 20-day average across megacaps, suggesting institutional repositioning rather than forced liquidation. The index lost the 6,800 handle early and has not looked back. Breadth is atrocious: 62 advancers vs 441 decliners in the SPX universe, a 1:7.1 ratio that screams capitulation-lite. Every sector in the red except scattered single-stock earnings plays.

NDX −1.69% is underperforming slightly as memory and semis bear the brunt of the commodity-inflation repricing. The AI infrastructure trade is getting hit hardest — DELL −7.9%, MU −7.2%, AMAT −4.6%, AMD −4.2% — as surging energy costs raise the specter of memory component inflation and data center power cost escalation. The 10Y ticking up 3bp to 4.061% does not tell the full story — the 2-day move has been the largest since June. Key Single-Stock Narratives
DELL −7.9% ($153.55, dvol 0.8x avg)

The memory complex is repricing in real-time and DELL is the epicenter. Despite posting a blowout Q4 with a $43B AI server backlog and record ISG growth, the stock is giving it all back as surging energy prices raise the cost of every component in the AI supply chain. Memory chip costs (NAND, DRAM) are already elevated from the AI shortage, and a prolonged Hormuz disruption adds energy cost inflation on top. The stock rallied +3.7% yesterday on the earnings beat — today's −7.9% is a full round-trip and then some. RSI at 62 still has room to fall. The read-through for the entire AI infrastructure chain is negative: if energy costs stay elevated, margins compress across servers, storage, and networking.

TGT +5.7% (dvol 1.1x avg)

Target delivered exactly what the market needed from a battered retailer: a clean earnings beat that finally broke the losing streak. Q4 adj EPS of $2.44 crushed the $2.17 consensus, driven by strength in Food & Beverage, Beauty, and Toys. Digital comps +1.9% offset a −3.9% store traffic decline. The real catalyst was the FY26 guide of $7.50–$8.50 EPS with 20bp margin expansion — mgmt framing 2026 as a "next chapter of growth." Shares up 15.6% YTD vs SPX flat, and this beat may confirm the turn. Watch for COST earnings later this week as the next retail read.

BBY +5.6% ($59.47, dvol 1.7x avg)

Best Buy beat on earnings but missed on revs — the market is rewarding the margin expansion story. Adj EPS $2.61 vs $2.47 est, though revs of $13.81B missed by ~$66M. Comps were −0.8%, better than feared. Computing and mobile categories drove strength while home theater and appliances continued to drag. Mgmt guided FY27 comps of −1% to +1% — not inspiring, but the profitability trajectory (adj OI up to $695M from $690M) is the story. AI partnerships with OpenAI and Google for product discovery are early but notable. Volume at 1.7x average says institutional positioning is resetting.

MU −7.2% ($190.34, dvol 0.7x avg)

Micron is caught in the crossfire of the memory repricing. Samsung and SK Hynix got crushed in Seoul today (Kospi −7%), and that selling is bleeding into US memory names. The thesis is simple: memory chip prices have been elevated on AI demand, but surging energy costs threaten to compress margins even as demand stays strong. RSI at 45 is approaching oversold but not there yet. The broader memory trade (WDC −6.0%, SNDK −5.6%) suggests this is sector-wide, not name-specific.

NRG −7.1% (dvol 2.9x avg)

NRG Energy is the surprise laggard in utilities, down −7.1% on nearly 3x average volume. The IPP complex is repricing on fears that surging natural gas costs cannot be fully passed through to customers in the near-term. This is counterintuitive — you would think power generators benefit from higher energy — but the market is pricing contract structure risk and regulatory pushback on rate hikes. The utes sector avg of −1.42% masks enormous dispersion.

ALB −7.0% ($165.66, dvol 0.7x avg)

Albemarle is getting hit from multiple angles: Korea's Kospi collapse is dragging the entire lithium/EV supply chain, and the Global X Lithium ETF (LIT) is pacing for its worst day since March 2020. The Hormuz disruption adds shipping cost fears for lithium carbonate supply chains that run through Asia. RSI at 46 is neutral but the SMA5 at 180.66 shows the stock is well below its 5-day trend.

Hormuz Escalation — What to Watch

Day 4 of the US-Israel operation "Epic Fury" has turned the Strait of Hormuz into the market's central risk variable. Iran's Revolutionary Guard claims the Strait is closed and will fire on any vessel attempting passage. Over 14M barrels/day transited the Strait in 2025 — roughly a third of global seaborne crude. Barclays sees Brent at $100 if disruption persists; UBS flags $120 in a worst case. Second-order effects: European nat gas +33% on Qatar's Ras Laffan disruption, shipping rerouting around Africa adding 2–3 weeks to transit times. The succession vacuum in Tehran means there is no clear off-ramp for de-escalation. This could have legs.

Bottom Line

This is a geopolitical tape, full stop. The Hormuz closure has turned what was a manageable risk-off into a genuine stagflation scare, with crude repricing every asset in the book. The earnings signal from TGT and BBY is constructive — the consumer is not dead — but nobody cares about micro when macro is on fire. The key variable is whether Hormuz stays closed: if it does, Brent is headed to $100 and this selloff has another 3–5% of downside. If rhetoric softens overnight, the reversal will be violent. Fed speakers today will not matter unless they explicitly address energy inflation. Stay defensive, sell rips, keep powder dry for the dislocation trades in oversold names if we get a de-escalation headline. Do not hero-bid into a news-driven tape.
