Historical Events — March 10
Alexander Graham Bell Makes the First Telephone Call — 1876
Three days after receiving his patent, Alexander Graham Bell transmitted the first intelligible telephone message to his assistant Thomas Watson in the next room of their Boston laboratory. Bell wrote his father that evening predicting wires would one day be run into homes like water and gas pipes. The invention launched the telecommunications industry and eventually spawned AT&T, for decades the most valuable company on Earth.
Operation Meetinghouse — The Firebombing of Tokyo — 1945
334 B-29 Superfortresses dropped nearly 1,700 tons of incendiary bombs on Tokyo in the single deadliest air raid in human history. An estimated 100,000 civilians were killed in the resulting firestorm, which destroyed 16 square miles of the city — more immediate casualties than either atomic bomb would later inflict. The raid validated General Curtis LeMay’s low-altitude firebombing strategy and accelerated the endgame of the Pacific War.
Wilt Chamberlain Drops 70 Points on the Nationals — 1963
Less than a year after his legendary 100-point game, Wilt Chamberlain poured in 70 points for the San Francisco Warriors in a 163-148 loss to the Syracuse Nationals at Onondaga War Memorial. The game’s combined 311 points remain one of the highest-scoring contests in NBA history. Chamberlain averaged 44.8 points per game that season — a number no player has come close to matching in the six decades since.
Buffy the Vampire Slayer Premieres on The WB — 1997
Joss Whedon’s Buffy the Vampire Slayer debuted as a midseason replacement on The WB, launching one of the most influential television series of the late 1990s. The show blended horror, comedy, and teen drama around a female action hero and ran for seven seasons across two networks. It pioneered long-arc serialized storytelling, built one of TV’s first major online fandoms, and became a cultural touchstone that reshaped genre television.
The NASDAQ Composite Hits Its Dot-Com Peak — 2000
The NASDAQ Composite touched an intraday high of 5,048.62, marking the absolute zenith of the dot-com bubble after a 500% surge in five years fueled by frenzied speculation in internet stocks. From this peak, the index would lose 78% of its value over the next 30 months, wiping out roughly $5 trillion in market capitalization. The NASDAQ would not reclaim its March 10, 2000 closing level for another 15 years — a reminder that valuation gravity is patient but undefeated.
