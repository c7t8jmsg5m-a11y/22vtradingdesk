Historical Events — March 19
AOL Goes Public at $11.50 a Share — 1992
America Online listed on the Nasdaq at $11.50 per share, closing at $14.75 — a 28% pop that valued the company at just $62 million. Within eight years, AOL would ride the dial-up internet boom to a peak market cap north of $200 billion and engineer the $165 billion merger with Time Warner, still considered one of the worst deals in corporate history. The IPO marked the starting gun of the dot-com era and the birth of the consumer internet as an investable asset class.
U.S. Launches Air Strikes on Baghdad — 2003
President George W. Bush ordered cruise missile and precision bomb strikes on Baghdad in a "decapitation attack" aimed at Saddam Hussein, launching the Iraq War. The S&P 500 had already priced in the invasion, rallying 3.5% the week prior, and oil spiked above $30 a barrel before falling as the initial campaign moved faster than expected. The war would last eight years, cost over $2 trillion, and reshape Middle Eastern geopolitics and the global energy map for a generation.
Woodrow Wilson Signs the Standard Time Act — 1918
President Wilson signed the Standard Time Act, establishing time zones and Daylight Saving Time across the United States as a wartime fuel conservation measure. The law standardized the patchwork of local times that had plagued railroad scheduling and interstate commerce since the 1880s. DST proved so unpopular with farmers that Congress repealed it the following year over Wilson's veto, though it returned during World War II and eventually became permanent seasonal practice.
Michael Jordan Returns to the Chicago Bulls — 1995
Two words — "I'm back" — ended the most dramatic retirement in sports history as Michael Jordan suited up for the Bulls for the first time in 21 months, wearing number 45 instead of his iconic 23. He scored 19 points in a 103-96 loss to the Indiana Pacers in Indianapolis, with the game drawing the highest NBA regular-season ratings in years. Jordan's return triggered a surge in Nike and Bulls merchandise sales and foreshadowed the 72-win dynasty season that followed in 1995-96.
Bob Dylan Releases His Debut Album — 1962
Columbia Records released "Bob Dylan," a 13-track collection of folk covers and two originals recorded in a single afternoon for a reported $402 in studio costs. The album sold only 5,000 copies in its first year, earning Dylan the nickname "Hammond's Folly" after the A&R man who signed him, John Hammond. Within two years Dylan would release "The Freewheelin' Bob Dylan" and "The Times They Are a-Changin'," becoming the voice of a generation and eventually selling more than 125 million records worldwide.
