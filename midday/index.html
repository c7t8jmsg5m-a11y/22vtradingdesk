Market Overview

SPX traded through the 6,710 support we flagged last week and is now testing the November lows near 6,690. The index is firmly below its 5-day (6,776), 20-day, and 50-day moving averages. Momentum is clearly negative — this is the third consecutive red session and the fifth in the last six. The selling has broadened out: only two of eleven GICS sectors — Energy and Utilities — are green at midday, with the remaining nine printing losses between 0.8% and 1.9%. Sector Narratives

Energy is the undisputed leader as the Iran-Hormuz conflict intensifies. With tankers being struck near the strait and Iran signaling no intention of ending the closure, Brent briefly hit $100 and WTI is trading near $95. Fertilizer and chemicals names are the biggest beneficiaries — CF +12.6% is exploding on input-cost pass-through expectations (a significant portion of fertilizer precursors transit Hormuz). DOW +8.3%, LYB +8.1%, and MOS +7.7% are along for the ride. Refiners are also bid: PSX +4.5%, VLO +3.3%, MPC +3.5%. E&Ps getting a lift with OXY +6.1% (Wells Fargo double-upgraded to OW with $69 PT on Permian productivity), CVX +3.2%, COP +3.0%. The XLE is at a new 2026 high — its 15th record intraday this year.

Financials are bleeding from the private credit contagion. MS −4.2% after gating its North Haven PIF (fulfilled only 46% of redemption requests). GS −3.9%, BLK −2.7%. This is the second-order contagion channel — AI disruption fears are hitting software borrower credit quality, which flows into private credit NAVs. Regional banks also soft: BAC −3.3%, C −3.3%, RF −3.1%.

Industrials are the worst sector at −1.9% average. GE Aerospace −6.7% is the big mover, with the oil spike raising fuel costs and depressing travel demand expectations — the same forces hammering cruise lines (CCL −6.5%, RCL −6.1%, LUV −5.8%). EMR −4.6%, BA −4.0% adding to the industrial malaise. The only bright spot is defense: NOC +0.9%, LMT +0.7%, GD flat — the Palantir/GE Air Force partnership expansion is a tailwind for the complex.

Key Single-Stock Narratives

CF Industries +12.6% — The fertilizer producer is surging on Hormuz transit disruption fears. A significant portion of ammonia and urea precursors pass through the Strait, and with tankers refusing to cross, the supply squeeze is real. Volume running 2.4x the 30-day average. RSI was already at 81 coming in — this is pure momentum chasing. The entire ag-chem complex is bid: MOS +7.7%, ADM +3.8%. The trade here is how long the Hormuz closure persists — if it extends past a week, these names have considerably more upside.

OXY +6.1% — Wells Fargo double-upgraded Occidental to Overweight with a $69 PT, citing stronger Permian Basin productivity and improved capital intensity. With WTI at $95, the math on preferred share redemption accelerates — if oil stays elevated, Buffett’s preferred can be retired in H2, unlocking more shareholder returns. Volume at 1.6x average. RSI was already at 74 — getting overbought but the fundamental catalyst is real.

CRM +3.2% — Salesforce continues its post-earnings rally two weeks after reporting a blowout Q4 (EPS $3.81 vs $3.04 est, revenue $11.2B +12% YoY). The $50B buyback announcement and 16% cRPO growth are keeping buyers engaged. Agentforce ARR near $2B run-rate. One of the few tech names green today — a flight-to-quality within software.

GE Aerospace −6.7% — The worst name in industrials today. The oil spike is a double whammy for aerospace: higher jet fuel costs crimp airline profitability (their customers) while geopolitical escalation threatens air travel demand. GE was already weak after providing soft revenue guidance in January, and the stock is now down to $303 from a $348 high. The Palantir partnership expansion for Air Force readiness is a long-term positive but isn’t moving the needle today against the macro headwinds. RSI at 36.

DG −5.5% — Dollar General reported a monster Q4 beat (EPS $1.93 vs $1.64 est, revenue $10.9B, comps +4.3%) but the stock is selling off on cautious FY26 guidance: sales growth of just 3.7%–4.2% and EPS of $7.10–$7.35 vs $7.17 consensus. After a 100%+ move off 52-week lows, investors are taking profits on a “sell the news” setup. Management cited inflationary headwinds on the core consumer and WOTC expiration as EPS drags.

MS −4.2% — Morgan Stanley’s stock is under pressure after the firm gated withdrawals from its North Haven Private Income Fund, fulfilling only 46% of investor tender requests. This follows BlackRock’s HLEND and Blackstone’s BCRED both limiting redemptions. The private credit gating story is becoming systemic — Deutsche Bank flagged $30B in exposure, JPM is restricting software-backed lending. Default rates approaching 9% for some funds. The entire alt manager complex (ARES −5.4%, BX −3.6%, APO −4.0%) is a no-touch until the redemption cycle stabilizes.

Bottom Line

This is a tape under siege from two directions: an oil shock that hasn’t peaked (Brent at $100, Hormuz still closed, no ceasefire signals) and a private credit stress cycle that’s spreading faster than expected (three major funds now gating, DB flagging exposure, JPM pulling back on software lending). The IEA’s record 400M barrel SPR release isn’t moving the needle — physical supply disruption trumps strategic reserves. The stagflation setup is real: oil higher, yields higher, growth expectations lower. The only places to hide are energy (which is overbought but has the fundamental wind), utilities (31/31 advancers today), and defense. Stay away from financials until the private credit redemption cycle burns out. Don’t try to be a hero on travel or consumer discretionary. Stay defensive, trade the singles in energy, and keep powder dry for when Hormuz headlines break the other direction.
