Historical Events — March 13
Disney Names Bob Iger as CEO, Beginning a Two-Decade Transformation — 2005
The Walt Disney Company board officially announced that Robert Iger, the company's president and COO, would succeed Michael Eisner as chief executive. Iger inherited a company in turmoil — fractured relationships with Pixar, stagnant theme park investment, and a stock price that had gone nowhere for years. Over the next two decades he would acquire Pixar, Marvel, Lucasfilm, and 21st Century Fox for a combined $70+ billion, transforming Disney from an aging media conglomerate into the dominant entertainment empire on earth.
The Anschluss: Nazi Germany Formally Annexes Austria — 1938
One day after German troops crossed the Austrian border unopposed, Nazi Chancellor Arthur Seyss-Inquart signed the law officially incorporating Austria into the Third Reich. Hitler, who had been born just across the border in Braunau am Inn, rode a wave of cheering crowds from Linz to Vienna, where 200,000 gathered at the Heldenplatz to hear him declare his homeland the newest province of Greater Germany. The annexation erased an independent nation of nearly seven million people overnight and proved to the world that Britain and France would not challenge Hitler's territorial ambitions — emboldening the aggression that led to World War II.
William Herschel Discovers the Planet Uranus — 1781
German-born British astronomer William Herschel, surveying the night sky with a homemade telescope from his garden in Bath, England, spotted an object he initially described as a "curious either nebulous star or perhaps a comet." Further observation confirmed it was a planet — the first to be discovered in modern history and the first found with a telescope. Herschel named it Georgium Sidus in honor of King George III; the astronomical community eventually settled on Uranus, doubling the known size of the solar system in a single evening.
Holyfield-Lewis Fight Ends in Controversial Draw — 1999
Evander Holyfield and Lennox Lewis met at Madison Square Garden in a long-awaited heavyweight unification bout, and after 12 rounds most ringside observers believed Lewis had clearly outboxed Holyfield. The split-draw decision stunned the arena and triggered an immediate uproar — one judge scored it 115-113 for Lewis, another 115-113 for Holyfield, and the third called it even at 113-113. The result was so controversial that investigations followed, and the rematch eight months later saw Lewis win by unanimous decision to become undisputed heavyweight champion of the world.
Eric Clapton Quits the Yardbirds — 1965
Twenty-year-old guitarist Eric Clapton walked out of the Yardbirds, disgusted that the band was drifting from raw blues toward commercial pop after recording the hit "For Your Love." On its face it was one man leaving one band, but the departure triggered a chain reaction that reshaped rock music for a generation. Clapton recommended Jimmy Page as his replacement; Page declined, and Jeff Beck got the job instead. Page eventually joined in 1966, and when the Yardbirds collapsed in 1968, he formed Led Zeppelin — meaning one blues purist's exit launched three of the greatest guitar careers in history.
